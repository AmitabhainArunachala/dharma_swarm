## Plain Thesis

Before DGC sells a grand story, it should prove one narrow thing:

**DGC can run a real revenue campaign for itself, hold context across days, and ship artifacts that cause paid work to happen.**

That means the first proving ground should not be:

- quant trading
- crypto trading
- a random micro-SaaS unrelated to the core moat

Those paths may make money, but they do not cleanly prove DGC's actual edge.

The correct first proving ground is:

**a DGC-native venture studio that uses the campaign ledger to create, sell, and deliver one narrow high-context operating product.**

## The Hard Decision

The first business should be:

`Campaign Ledger OS for AI-native teams, sold first as a managed deployment and only later as software`

This is the public-neutral name.

Internal or dharmic name:

`Shakti Foundry`

That pairing matters.

- `Campaign Ledger OS` is legible to buyers.
- `Shakti Foundry` preserves the deeper truth that the system is trying to turn diffuse intelligence into directed force.

## Why This Wins Over Trading Or A Random SaaS

### Trading is a bad first proof

- it proves market luck or strategy alpha more than DGC's actual moat
- it demands risk, compliance, and execution discipline that pull the architecture away from memory and artifact production
- a small win looks noisy; a loss destroys trust fast
- even if it works, future customers cannot clearly infer that DGC can run their org better

### Random micro-SaaS is better than trading but still weak

- it often forces shallow feature work disconnected from DGC's strongest capabilities
- it can become another generic AI wrapper
- it risks optimizing for signup novelty instead of continuity, evidence, and real operator leverage

### Campaign Ledger OS is the clean proof

- you already need it internally
- it strengthens the core DGC substrate directly
- it creates a usable external offer quickly
- it lets service revenue fund product hardening
- it can later support the larger Jagat Kalyan / commons arc instead of distracting from it

## The Core Object

The first real commercial object is:

The product is:

## What Actually Gets Sold First

The first ladder should be:

1. `Campaign X-Ray`
   A paid diagnostic that ingests the buyer's mess and returns one ranked brief packet, one bottleneck map, and one recommended campaign.
2. `Campaign Sprint`
   A 1-2 week engagement that turns the chosen campaign into one real artifact and one proof packet.
3. `Campaign Desk`
   A monthly continuation that keeps the campaign ledger live and keeps outputs flowing.

This is the same economic spine as the earlier `Mission X-Ray -> Mission Sprint -> Continuous Mission Desk` framing, but sharpened around a single internal proving system.

## Who Pays First

The first paying buyers should be:

1. `AI-native founders`
   One founder, one messy product surface, too many notes, too many branches, not enough continuity.
2. `Small AI labs or research studios`
   Three to twenty people, fast-moving, drowning in documents, experiments, and provider churn.
3. `Technical founder offices`
   A founder plus a few operators who need synthesis, prioritization, and artifact production more than another dashboard.

Do not start with:

- giant enterprises
- compliance-heavy buyers
- pure content agencies
- high-frequency trading teams

The first proof needs high pain, low procurement friction, and buyers who can feel the value inside one week.

## Main Income Streams

Order matters.

### Stream 1: Managed Campaign Revenue

This is the first and most important stream.

You get paid to:

- ingest the buyer's world
- compile the first campaign
- coordinate execution
- return artifacts and evidence

This is the fastest route to cash and the fastest route to hardening DGC in reality.

### Stream 2: Continuation Revenue

Once the buyer sees continuity value, the desk becomes sticky.

The real recurring revenue comes from:

- weekly priority packets
- active campaign steering
- memory continuity
- verified handoffs

### Stream 3: Product Revenue

Only after repeated managed deployments should you charge for the software layer itself.

The SaaS wedge is not the whole DGC vision.
It is the repeatable shell around the parts that prove stable.

### Stream 4: Internal Venture Revenue

Later, DGC can launch its own narrow products or data/tools from inside the studio.
But that should be downstream of a working operator system, not the first bet.

### Stream 5: Commons / Reciprocity Revenue

Longer term, the cash engine funds the larger public-benefit and dharmic arc.
That is mission-consistent only if the commercial engine becomes real first.

## The Selection Math

To keep this from becoming intuition theater, use a simple score for target accounts.

`PartnerScore = 0.30 Pain + 0.20 Urgency + 0.20 CorpusDensity + 0.15 BuyingAuthority + 0.10 ProofLeverage + 0.05 TrustFit`

Each variable should be scored `1-5`.

Definitions:

- `Pain`: how badly the buyer is suffering from context loss, fragmentation, or blocked execution
- `Urgency`: whether a decision or artifact is needed this month, not someday
- `CorpusDensity`: whether the buyer has enough repo/doc/note mass for DGC to outperform generic chat tools
- `BuyingAuthority`: whether the person can actually say yes
- `ProofLeverage`: whether a successful engagement becomes a high-value case study
- `TrustFit`: whether the buyer will tolerate an honest frontier product instead of demanding fake certainty

Use a second score for which offer to lead with.

`OfferPriority = WinProbability x ProofValue x ACV / DeliveryRisk`

This forces discipline.
The first customer should not only be "who might pay most."
It should be "who creates the strongest next proof loop."

## The Working System DGC Must Have Internally

Before selling aggressively, DGC should be able to run this loop on itself:

1. ingest one active internal mission
2. compile one campaign ledger
3. produce one morning brief and one weekly revenue packet
4. delegate bounded tasks
5. generate one verified artifact
6. update memory and evidence
7. resume coherently the next day

The working system threshold is:

- `raw corpus -> first brief` in under `24 hours`
- `brief -> first artifact` in under `72 hours`
- `verification pass rate` above `80%` for promised deliverables
- `human re-explaining overhead` trending down week over week
- `one buyer-facing demo` that can be run without hiding the brittle parts

If those conditions are not true, DGC is not yet a working system.
It is still an interesting prototype.

## Design-Partner Pricing

Keep the first three engagements simple.

### Introductory design-partner prices

- `Campaign X-Ray`: `$3k-$6k`
- `Campaign Sprint`: `$12k-$20k`
- `Campaign Desk`: `$6k-$12k/month`

### Standardized prices after proof

- `Campaign X-Ray`: `$5k-$12k`
- `Campaign Sprint`: `$15k-$35k`
- `Campaign Desk`: `$12k-$40k/month`

Why the temporary discount exists:

- the early buyer is partly paying for delivery and partly helping harden the operator system
- you need speed of close and fast proof more than peak margin on the first few deals

Do not discount by saying "we are early."
Discount by saying:

`This is design-partner pricing in exchange for fast feedback and a tighter collaboration loop.`
